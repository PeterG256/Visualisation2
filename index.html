Deadly Volcano Eruptions

A volcanic eruption is a natural disaster where materials such as rocks and particles, called tephra, are expelled out from a volcano. An eruption by itself is enough to kill directly, though after a powerful enough eruption, the surrounding air and ground may become toxic, causing crops to fail and breathing to be difficult among other things.
The Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI) is measure of how potent an eruption is. It is a logarithmic scale from 0 (where no tephra are released from the volcano) to 8 (where 1,000,000,000,000 m³ of tephra are released).

Where and have the most fatal volcanic eruptions been, and what kind of volcano erupted?

Evidentally, the areas with the most fatal volcanic activity are Indonesia, Phillipines, Japan, and Central America. There have been eruptions with a VEI of 8, but none since humanity started recording history. Most volcanic eruptions are from stratovolcanos, which are the quintessential example of volcanoes (a single conical mountain covered by many layers of tephra with an opening at the top). The tallest volcano to have caused a death is Cotopaxi at 5,911 m. For comparison, the tallest mountain in the world Mount Everest is 8,848 m tall.

What are the eruptions with the most deaths?

The deadliest eruption was Tambora in Indonesia with a VEI of 7. It was so powerful that its tephra spread wide enough to lower the temperature globally, causing 1816 to be what is known as The Year Without Summer. There have been no other documented VEI 7 eruptions.

By which agents do eruptions cause the most deaths?

Of all ways to die from a volcanic eruption, pyroclastic flows are the biggest killer. They are a mix of volcanic matter travelling close to the ground that can reach temperatures of 1000°C and speeds of 100 km/h.
